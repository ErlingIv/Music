ℹ️ About this collection

A personal archive of Norwegian and Nordic sheet music, gathered, catalogued, and made freely available to play, print, and read.

What's here

This site is a growing archive of historical music scores from Norway and the wider Nordic region, spanning roughly the 1800s to the mid-1900s, alongside a smaller number of international works connected to them (through lyricists, dedications, or shared publishers). Most entries are compositions that have fallen into the public domain, each linked — where available — to a scanned PDF of the original score, an MP3 recording, and a MuseScore transcription.

Every composer and lyricist page gathers what's known about them: birth and death years, nationality, a short biography where one exists, and the works connected to them. Entries are added and corrected continually, so some biographical details (especially for lesser-known figures) remain incomplete or uncertain.

The colour dots on MuseScore links

Next to a composition's MuseScore link, a small coloured dot hints at how much background information (programme notes, history, context) has been written up for that piece on MuseScore:

Little or no written information yet.
A moderate amount of background notes.
Substantial background notes — worth a read.

The coloured stripe on composer cards

On the composer grid, a thin coloured stripe down the left edge of each card gives a quick sense of how many public domain works by that person are in the archive:

1 work catalogued so far.
2–5 works.
6–19 works.
20 or more works — the collection's most represented composers.

Design & colour palette

The site's look is deliberately paper-like — warm off-white backgrounds, a serif display face for titles, and a muted, ink-on-paper colour scheme meant to feel closer to an old songbook than a database.

Beyond the interface accents above, a few colours carry specific meaning: pink tones mark female composers and lyricists, blue marks nationality, and the red/gold/blue/grey stripes and dots described above encode catalogue size and how much background text exists for a piece — not aesthetic choices, but shorthand for information the full record would otherwise take a paragraph to say.

About the source material

Scores come from a mix of sources: physical copies, the National Library of Norway (Nasjonalbiblioteket), IMSLP, university archives, and private collections, among others. Where a composition's copyright status is uncertain, it's marked accordingly rather than assumed to be free to use — only works confirmed as public domain are presented as freely downloadable here.
